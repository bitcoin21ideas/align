# Philosophy

## The core bet

Most productivity tools optimise for capture volume. Align optimises for capture quality.

The dominant assumption in the market is that you should record everything and let software sort it out. Voice tools transcribe your words. AI tools summarize them. Search tools retrieve them. The user becomes a passive producer of raw material.

Align makes the opposite bet: **the act of conscious capture is itself the value.** Speaking a decision out loud — naming what you chose and why — forces a level of clarity that passive recording never produces. The output is a side effect of the thinking, not a substitute for it.

> Passive capture scales volume. Conscious capture scales clarity.

---

## Human-in-the-loop curation

**HITL** (human-in-the-loop) is not a technical constraint. It is a design principle.

You decide what to say. You decide what to record. The system processes what you give it and nothing more. There is no ambient recording, no automatic import from other tools, no passive logging of browser history or calendar events.

This has a practical consequence: every item in your memory and task system was put there intentionally. When you query it six months from now, you are not wading through noise — you are reading a curated signal that you produced yourself.

Auto-capture tools produce convenient noise. This system produces a trusted, typed signal that compounds over time.

> No ambient capture is not just a choice. It’s a filtering mechanism.

---

## Why walking monologues

Walking reduces friction to zero. There is no keyboard, no open tab, no context switching. You speak into a phone and keep moving.

The walking format also tends to produce better thinking than sitting at a desk. The lack of a screen means you cannot edit yourself in real time. You say what you actually think, not what looks good in a document.

The 15–30 minute monologue is the primary unit of input. Everything else — transcription, structuring, archiving — is downstream processing.

> Walking monologues are the *default mode for high-signal thinking*, not the only mode.

## The four outputs and why they exist

A single recording produces four distinct outputs because four different problems need solving:

**Daily note** — a structured record of what happened today. Searchable, queryable via Obsidian Dataview, useful for retrospectives.

**Rolling handover** — a living operational dashboard. This is the differentiating output. It answers the question "what is actually in flight right now?" not "what happened in the past." The 60-line cap forces triage: you cannot accumulate indefinitely. Items that are not worth defending get removed. Removed items go to the archive with a reason — they are never silently deleted.

**Archive** — an append-only record of everything that left the handover and why. Completed tasks, resolved questions, parked ideas, aged-out items. Auditable, lossless.

**Memory entries** — typed, structured extractions of the things most worth remembering across projects and time. Not tasks. Not questions. Decisions, constraints, mistakes, and patterns. The format forces the "why" alongside the "what."

---

## The rolling handover as the key differentiator

No other voice-to-note tool produces a living operational state document.

Most tools produce a growing pile of notes. The handover is the opposite: a fixed-size window on current reality. It is rewritten — not appended to — on every run. The AI does not add to a list; it performs aggressive triage, enforcing hard caps and explicit removal rules.

The result is a document you can actually act from. 60 lines. Always current. The discipline of the cap is the point.

> A system that doesn’t force removal becomes untrustworthy.

---

## The memory layer and why types matter

Memory entries are typed for a reason: `decision`, `constraint`, `mistake`, `pattern`.

Typing forces you to be specific about what kind of thing you are recording. A mistake entry requires you to name what went wrong and why — not just log that something happened. A constraint entry is a commitment that cannot be violated, not a preference or a wish.

The most valuable type is `mistake`. Mistakes that are named, structured, and stored are the ones you do not repeat. Unstructured retrospective notes are the ones you do.

> Most systems let you structure knowledge. This system forces you to structure judgment.

Memory entries compound. A year of weekly walks produces a typed record of your actual decision-making history — what you decided, what constraints you discovered, what patterns emerged across projects. That is genuinely useful context for future decisions, in a way that a pile of transcripts is not.

## Data ownership and privacy

All data lives in private GitHub repositories that you own. The pipeline is self-hostable. There is no SaaS database storing your thoughts.

This is not an incidental feature. Voice monologues contain information you would not put in a shared document. The system is designed so that you never have to choose between using it and trusting it.
